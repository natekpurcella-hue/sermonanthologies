# Historical Context for Sermons

### George Müller: The Secret of George Müller's Power
- **The Tidbit:** When a friend asked Müller for the "secret" of his success, the elderly man famously bent lower and lower until his head almost touched the floor. He then whispered that the secret was "dying to George Müller."
- **Context:** This internal "death" occurred in 1829 in Teignmouth, when he resolved to lay aside all his own academic opinions and fixed salary to rely solely on the Holy Spirit and the Bible. This surrendered life is what powered his ability to care for 10,000 orphans without ever asking for a donation.

### George Müller: Deepening Faith (1835)
- **The Tidbit:** At the start of this year, as Müller began feeling called to help orphans, he recorded that he had exactly two shillings in his pocket.
- **Context:** 1835 was a year of social crisis in England. The Poor Law of 1834 had just banned financial aid to the poor in their homes, forcing orphans into the "Workhouse"—a place so grim it was treated like a prison. Müller’s "Deepening Faith" was his response to this law; he wanted to prove to a cynical world that God would provide for these children if even one man simply took Him at His word.

### John Wesley: Salvation by Faith
- **The Tidbit:** This was Wesley’s "Manifesto." He preached it at St. Mary’s, Oxford, only 25 days after his famous Aldersgate experience (where his heart was "strangely warmed").
- **Context:** Imagine the scene: Wesley was preaching to the academic elite of Oxford who knew him as a rigid, legalistic priest. He stood before them to publicly "tear up" his old life of trying to earn God's favor, declaring instead that salvation was a free gift. It was so controversial that it eventually led to him being barred from most traditional pulpits.

### Charles Finney: The Prayer of Faith
- **The Tidbit:** This lecture was delivered in a converted theater (the Chatham Street Chapel) that was once a site for "worldly" entertainment.
- **Context:** Finney preached this in the winter of 1834 to save a struggling abolitionist newspaper. The chapel was a "nerve center" for the radical anti-slavery movement, and Finney’s message was clear: if Christians had the "faith" to pray for a revival, that revival would not only save souls but would also end national sins like slavery.

### John Wesley: Justification by Faith
- **The Tidbit:** In 1742, four years after writing this sermon, Wesley was forbidden from preaching in his father’s old church in Epworth. In response, he went into the churchyard, stood on his father’s tombstone, and preached this very sermon to the thousands who gathered.
- **Context:** By standing on the tombstone, Wesley was making a visual point: he was "standing on the shoulders" of the faith of his ancestors while declaring a "new" and radical message of justification that the institutional church of his day was too afraid to hear.
